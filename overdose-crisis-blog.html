Between 2016 and 2024, more than fifty thousand people in Canada died from opioid-related causes. The primary driver of this escalation is the contamination of the unregulated drug supply with illicitly manufactured fentanyl and its analogues—a supply-side crisis in which people who use drugs are exposed to substances of unpredictable potency, with no quality control and no margin for error (PHAC, 2025). These deaths are not evenly distributed: they cluster by province, by age, by sex, and by socioeconomic circumstance. This article examines the patterns in the data, the parallel trends in health system spending, and what the numbers can and cannot tell us about a crisis that continues to reshape communities across the country.

Methodological note: Percentage changes in this article (e.g., the 182% increase from 2016 to 2023) are sensitive to the choice of baseline year. Canada's national overdose surveillance was in its early stages in 2016, and some provinces may have underreported in the first year. Using 2017 as the baseline would yield a 104% increase. All rates presented here are crude rates (not age-standardized), which may overstate differences between provinces with different age structures. See Methods & Limitations for full discussion.

The trajectory reveals two distinct phases. From 2016 to 2019, deaths fluctuated between approximately 2,800 and 4,200 per year. The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 coincided with a sharp escalation to 6,061 deaths—a 71% increase from 2019. Public health measures, disrupted treatment access, isolation, and an increasingly toxic unregulated drug supply are all cited as contributing factors (PHAC, 2025). Deaths continued to climb to a peak of 7,997 in 2023 before declining modestly to 7,289 in 2024. Preliminary 2025 data (January through June) shows 2,787 deaths, suggesting a potential continued decline, though reporting lags mean this figure will likely be revised upward.
Note: 2025 data covers January to June only. Figures are subject to revision as provincial coroner and medical examiner reports are finalized (PHAC, 2025).

The proportion of opioid deaths involving fentanyl or fentanyl analogues nearly doubled between 2016 and 2021, fundamentally changing the nature of the crisis (PHAC, 2025).

stacked_bar_chart Fentanyl vs. Non-Fentanyl Involvement in Opioid Deaths
Percentage of opioid-related deaths by substance involvement, Canada • Categories overlap: a single death may involve both • Source: PHAC (2025)
The shift from pharmaceutical opioids to illicitly manufactured fentanyl represents the most significant transformation in Canada's overdose crisis. In 2016, fentanyl was involved in roughly half of opioid deaths. By 2021, that proportion had risen to 86%. The 2024 and preliminary 2025 data show a modest decline in fentanyl involvement (74% and 57%, respectively), though this may reflect changes in classification or reporting rather than a genuine shift in the drug supply. Critically, the "non-fentanyl opioid" category can exceed 100% when combined with fentanyl figures because deaths may involve multiple substances simultaneously.
Note: Percentages represent the proportion of opioid deaths in which each substance type was detected. Categories are not mutually exclusive; a single death may involve both fentanyl and non-fentanyl opioids. Totals may exceed 100% (PHAC, 2025).

The demographic concentration of opioid deaths is striking. Men account for more than three-quarters of all deaths, with the 30–39 age group bearing the heaviest burden (1,613 male deaths in 2023 alone). Women in the same age range experienced 674 deaths. The data also reveal that 96% of opioid deaths in 2023 were classified as accidental, with only 2% attributed to suicide and 1% to undetermined causes (PHAC, 2025). This classification is significant: it suggests that the vast majority of people who died did not intend to do so, pointing to a crisis of an increasingly toxic, unpredictable drug supply rather than one of intentional self-harm.
Critical data gap: Indigenous communities. The national PHAC surveillance data used in this analysis does not include Indigenous-specific breakdowns. However, provincial data reveal severe disparities. In British Columbia, First Nations people die from illicit drug toxicity at 5–6 times the rate of non-Indigenous people (British Columbia Coroners Service & First Nations Health Authority, 2024). These disparities are rooted in structural determinants including intergenerational trauma, the legacy of residential schools, inadequate on-reserve health services, and the intersection of housing instability with substance use. The absence of this data from national reporting should not be mistaken for the absence of the disparity. Additionally, socioeconomic factors—housing status, income, and recent incarceration—are strong predictors of overdose death risk but are not captured in this dataset. BC Coroners Service data consistently shows that the majority of overdose deaths occur in private residences, not in public spaces.

The provincial variation in opioid death rates is among the most striking features of the data. In 2023, British Columbia's crude rate of 40.2 per 100,000 was more than 11 times Quebec's crude rate of 3.5 per 100,000. Alberta closely followed BC at 39.8. Saskatchewan (28.8) and Manitoba (26.3) occupy an intermediate position, while Ontario (16.9) sits near the national average. These disparities reflect differences in drug supply composition, treatment infrastructure, harm reduction availability, and socioeconomic conditions—not simply differences in drug use prevalence (Canadian Centre on Substance Use and Addiction [CCSA], 2023). Important caveat: These are crude rates, not age-standardized. Because opioid deaths concentrate in the 20–49 age group, provinces with younger populations (e.g., Alberta, Saskatchewan) may appear to have higher rates partly due to demography rather than differential risk exposure alone. Age-standardized rates, where available, should be preferred for interprovincial comparison.

Deaths represent only the most severe outcome of the overdose crisis. Emergency department visits, hospitalizations, and emergency medical services responses all reflect the broader burden on Canada's health system. Each death recorded in the PHAC surveillance data represents a fraction of the non-fatal overdoses, treatment encounters, and emergency interventions that place cumulative strain on an already pressured system (Canadian Institute for Health Information [CIHI], 2024).
Provincial Spotlight

Quebec's Relative Protection

shield Quebec's opioid death rate is consistently 3–11 times lower than western provinces

Quebec's remarkably lower opioid death rate warrants careful interpretation. Several factors may contribute, though no single explanation is sufficient:

  • medication
    Opioid agonist treatment access. Quebec has one of Canada's most mature methadone and buprenorphine programs, with high prescribing rates relative to population (Institut national de santé publique du Québec [INSPQ], 2023).
  • science
    Drug supply composition. The unregulated drug supply in Quebec has historically contained less fentanyl than in British Columbia or Alberta, though this may be changing (CCSA, 2023).
  • gavel
    Classification differences. Provincial coroner practices and death classification criteria vary, which may partially account for rate differences. Quebec uses a medical examiner system with specific toxicological thresholds (PHAC, 2025).
  • warning
    Caution on interpretation. A lower death rate does not mean Quebec is unaffected. Quebec's stimulant-related harms have been rising, and the province experienced a 109% increase in opioid deaths in 2024 (645, up from 308 in 2023)—a signal that whatever factors previously limited the crisis may be eroding.

Stimulant-related deaths—primarily involving cocaine and methamphetamine—have followed a trajectory parallel to opioid deaths, rising from 2,357 in 2018 to 5,326 in 2023. Many of these deaths involve both stimulants and opioids simultaneously, reflecting the reality of polysubstance use in Canada's unregulated drug supply. The stimulant data underscores that the overdose crisis cannot be understood through an opioid-only lens (PHAC, 2025).

account_balance Overdose Deaths vs. Psychiatry Spending by Province
Large provinces, 2016–2024. Psychiatry data available 2021–2023 only • Sources: PHAC (2025); CIHI (2024)
When measured over the same time window (2021–2023), opioid deaths and psychiatry payments grew at nearly identical rates—approximately 8% each. The longer-term escalation in deaths (182% from 2016 to 2023) predates available specialty-level spending data, making direct trend comparison impossible. Critically, psychiatrist billing data is a poor proxy for addiction treatment spending. The majority of opioid agonist therapy (methadone, buprenorphine/Suboxone) is prescribed by family physicians and nurse practitioners, not psychiatrists. Community-based treatment, harm reduction services, supervised consumption sites, naloxone distribution, and addiction medicine programs are entirely absent from CIHI specialty payment data. The data presented here reflect psychiatrist clinical billing only—they do not and cannot measure the adequacy of Canada's addiction treatment response (CIHI, 2024).
Methodological note: CIHI physician payment data uses fiscal years (April–March) while PHAC overdose data uses calendar years (January–December). This temporal misalignment introduces approximately three months of offset in year-over-year comparisons. Psychiatry ranks as the fourth-highest medical specialty by total clinical payments in Canada, at approximately $1.9 billion in 2023. This represents 5.5% of all physician payments. Family medicine dominates at $15.4 billion (44.6%), reflecting its role as the primary point of contact for most Canadians. The specialty spending breakdown illustrates that psychiatry, while substantial in absolute terms, represents a modest share of total physician expenditure. Importantly, many addiction and mental health services are delivered outside of psychiatrist billing—through family physicians, nurse practitioners, counselors, and community organizations—and are not captured in these figures (CIHI, 2024).
Methods & Limitations

What the Data Cannot Tell Us

info Important limitations for interpreting this analysis
This article presents descriptive analysis of population-level surveillance data. It does not establish causal relationships between variables. All correlations—particularly between spending patterns and health outcomes—should be interpreted with caution.
  • Ecological fallacy
    Province-level comparisons cannot be used to draw conclusions about individual-level risk. A province with higher psychiatry spending may not direct that spending toward populations at highest overdose risk.
  • Temporal misalignment
    CIHI physician payment data uses fiscal years (April–March) while PHAC overdose surveillance uses calendar years (January–December). This introduces a three-month offset in comparisons.
  • Classification variation
    Provinces use different coroner and medical examiner systems with varying toxicological thresholds and classification practices. This affects cross-provincial comparisons of death rates (PHAC, 2025).
  • Reporting lag
    Recent data (particularly 2024 and 2025) is subject to revision as coroner investigations are completed. Apparent declines in the most recent periods may partly reflect incomplete reporting.
  • Unmeasured confounders
    Drug supply toxicity, naloxone distribution, safe consumption site availability, housing stability, and incarceration patterns are not captured in this dataset but are known to influence overdose outcomes.
  • Crude rates
    This analysis uses crude rates (deaths per 100,000 total population) rather than age-standardized rates. Given the concentration of deaths in the 20–49 age group, provinces with younger populations may appear to have higher rates independent of other risk factors.
  • Missing populations
    Indigenous-specific data is not reported in the PHAC national surveillance dataset used here, despite disproportionate harm documented in province-specific reports (British Columbia Coroners Service, 2024).
  • Spending ≠ service access
    Psychiatrist billing data reflects payments for services rendered, not the adequacy or accessibility of mental health care. Wait times, geographic distribution, and the scope of addiction-specific services are not captured.

Methodology

Data sources: PHAC Opioid- and Stimulant-Related Harms surveillance data (updated December 2025), covering January 2016 through June 2025. CIHI National Physician Database payments data for fiscal years 2021–2022 through 2023–2024.

Analysis: Descriptive statistics computed from publicly available aggregate data. No individual-level records were accessed. Provincial comparisons use crude rates as reported by PHAC. Physician payments reported in constant current-year dollars (not inflation-adjusted).

Data processing: Source data were ingested into a Databricks Unity Catalog environment following a Bronze (raw) → Silver (standardized) → Gold (star schema) medallion architecture. All transformations are documented and reproducible.

Preliminary 2025 data suggest a potential easing of the crisis, though it is too early to determine whether this reflects a durable trend or reporting artifacts. What the nine years of data examined here make clear is that the overdose crisis in Canada is not a uniform phenomenon—it varies profoundly by geography, by demography, and by substance.

Evidence-Based Responses: What the Data Does Not Show

This analysis focuses on mortality surveillance and physician spending. It does not capture the evidence-based interventions that have been deployed across Canada in response to the crisis, including:

  • health_and_safety
    Naloxone distribution. Take-home naloxone programs have reversed hundreds of thousands of overdoses since their expansion. The number of kits distributed nationally has grown substantially, though comprehensive distribution data is not included in this analysis.
  • local_hospital
    Supervised consumption and overdose prevention sites. Facilities such as Vancouver's Insite have operated for two decades with zero on-site deaths. Over 40 sanctioned sites now operate across Canada, though coverage remains limited relative to need (Health Canada, 2024).
  • medication
    Opioid agonist treatment (OAT). Methadone and buprenorphine/Suboxone programs remain the most evidence-supported treatment for opioid use disorder. Access has expanded but remains unevenly distributed, particularly in rural and northern communities.
  • science
    Safe supply and drug checking. Prescribed safe supply programs and community drug checking services represent emerging interventions aimed at reducing exposure to the toxic unregulated supply.

Effective responses will need to be as differentiated as the crisis itself. The data presented here can inform where and for whom interventions are most urgently needed—but the effectiveness of those interventions requires its own body of evidence.
